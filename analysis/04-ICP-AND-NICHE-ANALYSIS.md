## First 3 Outreach Sprints I Would Actually Run

### 1. Phoenix, Arizona + pool service

Why first:

- demand is obvious,
- recurring-service economics are good,
- sites are often functional but weak,
- owners usually publish phone, email, or both,
- the niche translates cleanly into a template.

### 2. Charlotte, North Carolina + tree service

Why second:

- Charlotte is one of the fastest numeric gainers in the 2024 Census release,
- tree jobs have meaningful lead value,
- before/after visuals and trust signals matter,
- many operators still run dated local sites.

### 3. Raleigh, North Carolina + garage door repair

Why third:

- the niche is more direct-response than design-sensitive,
- “call now / book repair” CTAs are easy to improve,
- it gives you a non-Florida, non-pool test with a different buying trigger.

## What to Avoid First

### De-prioritize HVAC, plumbing, and roofing for wave 1

They are real markets, but for your first 90 days they are worse markets than they look because:

- more ad spend,
- more established agencies,
- more franchise or bigger regional operators,
- stronger review / local SEO arms race.

You can absolutely go there later. They are just not the easiest place to validate a new outbound engine.

## Why These Pairs Work for an Automated Pipeline

Your pipeline works best when all of these are true:

- site weakness is visible without deep discovery,
- the business publishes clear contact info,
- the service area is narrow enough to personalize,
- the value of a single lead is high enough to justify `>$79/mo`,
- the niche can use a repeatable template without becoming generic.

That is why `pool service`, `tree service`, `garage door`, and selective `landscaping` beat broader “every local business” targeting.

## Operational Note

Treat these as ranking hypotheses, not permissions to blast. Your own SOP is correct:

1. mini-validate the pair,
2. confirm `>=60%` contactability,
3. confirm `>=50%` weak-site rate,
4. only then launch the sprint.
