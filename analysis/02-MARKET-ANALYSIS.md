## TAM / SAM / SOM for U.S. Local-Business Website Services

### Bottom line

The U.S. market is large enough. Your constraint is not TAM. Your constraint is niche selection, response rates, and operational discipline.

| Layer | Method | Estimated opportunity |
|---|---|---:|
| Broad TAM: U.S. SMB website demand | `34.0M` SMBs in the U.S.; `17%` still have no website | `5.78M` greenfield prospects |
| Broad refresh TAM | `83%` have a website; `90%` plan to invest; `58%` prioritize redesign / visual refresh | about `14.7M` redesign-intent SMBs |
| Broad active demand pool | Greenfield + redesign-intent pool | about `20.5M` SMBs |
| Dollar TAM at your recurring price band | `20.5M x $708-$2,988 ARR equivalent` | about `$14.5B-$61.2B` |
| Narrow SAM: core local-service niches | latest published counts across plumbing, HVAC, roofing, landscaping, pest, pool, tree, electrical | about `767k` businesses |
| Dollar SAM at your recurring price band | `767k x $708-$2,988` | about `$543M-$2.29B` |
| Near-term SOM | first `25-100` clients at your current launch pricing | roughly `$24k-$155k ARR` |

### What this means

- There is enough market to build a real business.
- You do **not** need to solve “all SMB websites.”
- Your viable path is to win a tiny share of a narrow, messy local-service segment faster than broader platforms can.

## Website Gap Analysis

### Hard numbers

- `83%` of U.S. small businesses now have a website.
- `17%` do **not**.
- `12%` launched a site in the last year.
- `45%` outsource website work to agencies.
- `41%` use no-code tools like Wix or Squarespace.
- `34%` use low-code tools like WordPress or Shopify.
- Only `12%` use custom development.

### How many have no website or a clearly weak one?

This is where the public data gets messy. There is no clean 2026 national dataset for “bad local-service websites.” The defensible answer is:

- **No website:** `17%` of SMBs.
- **Clearly weak website:** likely another `25%-35%` of local service SMBs.

That `25%-35%` is an **inference**, not an official published stat. It is based on:

- `58%` of SMBs planning a full redesign or visual refresh,
- `53%` prioritizing speed / performance work,
- `45%` prioritizing mobile improvements,
- `70%` of small-business websites reportedly lacking a clear homepage CTA, as summarized by Clutch from Sweor.

### Working estimate for your ICP

For local service businesses, a realistic working assumption is:

| Segment | Estimated share |
|---|---:|
| No website | `17%` |
| Website exists but weak enough to sell against | `25%-35%` |
| Total reachable pain pool | about `42%-52%` |

That is the real reason your model can work: not because websites are rare, but because a large minority of local service businesses still have weak conversion surfaces.

## Market Growth Trends

## YoY growth in “website for small business” demand

There is no trustworthy official 2026 benchmark for that exact phrase. The best current demand proxies are:

- `12%` of SMBs launched a website in the last 12 months.
- `90%` of SMBs plan to invest in their website over the next 12 months.

For strategy purposes, I would treat that as **high-single-digit to low-double-digit annual demand growth** for SMB website creation / refresh, with the strongest growth in redesigns, performance upgrades, and AI-assisted rebuilds rather than first-ever sites.

## Secular shifts that matter

| Trend | Current signal | Why it matters to you |
|---|---|---|
| AI and no-code mainstreaming | `41%` no-code, `34%` low-code | Lowers the cost floor and kills commodity brochure-site pricing |
| Outsourcing still meaningful | `45%` outsource to agencies | There is still room for done-for-you service if the offer is clear |
| Website investment remains active | `90%` plan website investment | The category is not shrinking |
| Search discovery is fragmenting | `40%` actively use generative AI in search; `15%` start local search in Maps | A website alone is weaker, but an owned site tied to Maps / reviews is still important |
| Websites still drive lead capture | `40%` of SMBs with a site say SEO is their top lead source | Search visibility still converts when the site is competent |

## Key Market Dynamics

### 1. The greenfield market is smaller than most agencies assume

You are not entering a world where most businesses still need their first site. You are entering a world where many already have *some* site, but a meaningful chunk of those sites are stale, generic, or conversion-poor.

### 2. Google Business Profile is not replacing the website

It is replacing part of the *discovery* job. BrightLocal found:

- `45%` of consumers default to Google for local search,
- `15%` default directly to Google Maps,
- `67%` often or always look at reviews after a local search,
- `85%` say contact information and opening hours matter when researching a local business.

That means your site is increasingly the **trust + conversion layer**, not the first discovery point.

### 3. The category is bifurcating

The market is splitting into:

- cheap DIY software,
- expensive full-service agencies,
- a middle lane for productized, done-for-you local-service websites.

That middle lane is your opening.

### 4. The best niches are not the biggest niches

HVAC, plumbing, and roofing are large, but they are obvious. The better first targets are niches where:

- lead value is still meaningful,
- service areas are clear,
- site weakness is visible,
- owners publish contact details,
- competition is not completely agency-saturated.
