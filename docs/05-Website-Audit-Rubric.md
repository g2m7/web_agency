# Website Audit Rubric

## Purpose

This rubric makes lead qualification and outreach specific. It prevents vague pitches like “your site could be better” and replaces them with sharp, visible issues.

## Scoring categories

Use a 0–2 scale for each category:

- 0 = acceptable
- 1 = weak
- 2 = clearly bad

## Audit categories

### Mobile experience
- Layout breaks on phone
- Text too small
- Buttons hard to tap
- Navigation difficult on mobile

### Speed and responsiveness
- Slow first load
- Heavy images
- Cluttered homepage
- Delayed interaction

### Trust and credibility
- Outdated look
- Poor-quality images
- Missing testimonials or reviews
- No visible trust indicators
- Broken SSL or security warnings

### Conversion structure
- No strong CTA
- Contact form hard to find
- Phone number buried
- No service-area clarity
- Weak offer framing

### Content quality
- Thin copy
- Old promotions or stale details
- Grammar issues
- Missing service explanations
- Missing FAQs

### Information architecture
- Confusing navigation
- Important pages missing
- Repetitive layout blocks
- No logical page hierarchy

### Business fit signals
- Good reviews but weak site
- Strong service business with poor digital polish
- Multiple services that can be organized better
- Evidence that a better site could improve lead handling

## Red-flag findings to mention in outreach

Use only one or two of these per email:

- Site breaks on mobile
- No clear CTA above the fold
- Homepage feels outdated or cluttered
- No clear service structure
- Weak trust signals despite strong reviews
- Website likely losing mobile visitors
- Contact path is harder than it should be

## Audit output format

For each lead, the agent records:

- Numeric score
- Top 3 issues
- One sentence explaining why the issue matters commercially
- One sentence describing how the new version improves it
- Lead priority tier (hot / warm / low)

The human operator periodically reviews agent audit quality to ensure scoring accuracy and commercial language quality.

## Commercial translation rule

Every technical problem must be translated into business language.

Examples:

- “Mobile layout breaks” becomes “people searching on their phone may leave before contacting you.”
- “No CTA hierarchy” becomes “the site does not direct visitors toward calling or booking.”
- “Outdated design” becomes “the site can make a legitimate business look less credible than it is.”

## Good outreach issue selection

Pick issues that are:

- Visibly true
- Easy to explain
- Hard for the prospect to deny
- Directly tied to trust or conversions

## Bad outreach issue selection

Avoid leading with:

- Highly technical jargon
- Minor accessibility tweaks
- Deep SEO points in the first message
- Anything that requires a long explanation
